Mozilla Funds Program to Put Austin's Gigabit Connections to Use
by Karl Bode
Thursday May 19 2016 08:30 EDT

Mozilla is funding a new effort in Austin exploring just what can be done with a gigabit. Over the last few years Austin has become one of the few hotbeds of broadband competition in the United States, with Google Fiber, AT&T, Grande Communications all now offering gigabit broadband for $65 per month and up. In the hopes of answer the age of question of "what should you do with all that speed," Mozilla says the organization is expending the Mozilla Gigabit Community Fund to Austin.


The Fund is a joint initiative between Mozilla, the National Science Foundation, and US Ignite, a group dedicated to coordinating use and development of ultra-fast broadband and the technologies and services such speeds enable.

In a blog post, Mozilla says it will be providing $150,000 in grants to local Austin projects and tools that leverage the city’s Google Fiber network. Among the technologies being explored are 4K streaming in classrooms, and immersive virtual reality for work (including real-time water quality monitoring systems and first responder tools), play and education.

“Selected from a list of contenders from across the country, Austin stood out due to its existing city-wide digital inclusion plan, active developer community, and growing informal education landscape," Mozilla’s Executive Director Mark Surman says of the effort.

“When you couple lightning-fast Internet with innovative projects in the realms of education and workforce development, amazing things can happen.”

Mozilla has funded similar initiatives in Chattanooga, Tennessee and Kansas City (there's a little more detail here), and says it will be expanding the Gigabit Community Fund to three additional gigabit cities by 2018.

Mozilla will open the first round of grant applications in Austin this August, and accept applications through October 18, 2016. Mozilla says applicants and projects don’t have to be from Austin originally, but must be piloted locally. Interested developers can read about the RFP process here.

Rightscorp Losing Money as Users Embrace Proxies, VPNs
by Karl Bode
Thursday May 19 2016 07:30 EDT

For a few years now Digital Rights Corp (aka Rightscorp) has been trying to turn copyright infringement notices into a revenue stream, sending accused pirates letters telling them they can avoid court battles if they just pay a $20-$30 fee. The group has run into numerous legal problems with it's en masse attempts at driving settlements, with numerous ISPs accusing the organization of being little more than a shake down operation and fighting the organization in court.

But Rightscorp's attempt to turn piracy into cash appears to be having problems: namely it's not making money.

Rightscorp's latest financial data indicates that the company generated just $68,283 in revenue last quarter, after posting a $3.5 million net loss last year.
